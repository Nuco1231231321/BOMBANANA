## The Blind Monkey 🙈

**Role:** *"See No Evil"*

The Blind Monkey is the **only character who can physically touch and interact with the bomb**. This is both the most stressful and the most critical role — you are the hands that defuse the bomb, but your eyes tell you almost nothing.

![BOMBANANA! Gameplay](/images/screenshots/ss_858b009c283bfaac07f526309cb8a68c0c284278.thumb.jpg)

### What You Can Do

- **Touch the bomb** — You are the only player who can cut wires, press buttons, flip switches, and rotate knobs
- **Hear voice chat** — You can hear every word your Deaf teammate says
- **Speak** — You can ask questions, repeat instructions back for confirmation, and report what you feel

### What You Cannot Do

- **See colors** — Every single wire looks exactly the same shade of gray to you
- **Read text** — Labels, numbers, screen readouts, module names — all invisible
- **Identify modules visually** — You cannot tell a wire panel from a button grid by sight alone

## The 3-Step Protocol

Every action follows this cycle:

### 1. LISTEN
When the Deaf gives an instruction, stop everything and listen completely. Do not anticipate. Do not guess.

### 2. REPEAT
Always repeat the instruction back before acting:
- Deaf: *"Cut the third wire from the left"*
- You: *"Third wire from left — confirm?"*
- Deaf: *"Confirmed — cut it"*

### 3. EXECUTE
Only after confirmation, perform the action. Narrate what you feel:
- *"Touching the third wire now... it feels thin... cutting now."*

This tactile feedback helps the Deaf and Mute correct your position if needed.

## Building Your Mental Map

Since you cannot see clearly, you must build a mental picture through touch:

### Initial Sweep (First 10 Seconds)

1. Place hands at the top-left corner of the bomb
2. Slowly sweep across the entire surface
3. Count distinct module shapes by feeling their boundaries
4. Report: *"I count 4 modules — starting sweep"*

### Tactile Signatures of Module Types

| Module Type | What You Feel |
|-------------|---------------|
| Wire Panel | Multiple thin, flexible lines in a row |
| Button Grid | Grid of raised circles or squares |
| Keypad | 3×3 or 4×3 arrangement of numbered buttons |
| Rotary Dial | Single raised circle that spins |
| Toggle Switches | Row of flip switches with distinct up/down positions |
| Touch Screen | Flat, smooth rectangle — you will need step-by-step guidance here |

### Spatial Language

Agree on these terms with your team before the bomb:

- **Compass directions**: Top-Left, Top-Right, Bottom-Left, Bottom-Right, Center
- **Numbered modules**: Module 1, 2, 3, 4 (left to right, top to bottom)
- **Wire numbering**: "First wire from left," "Second wire from right"

## Wire Cutting: The Critical Operation

One wrong cut can detonate the bomb. Follow this exact procedure:

1. Deaf instructs: *"Cut the second wire from the left"*
2. Run your fingers along the wires from left to right, counting aloud
3. Stop on the target wire, pinch it between two fingers
4. Report: *"I am holding the second wire from the left"*
5. Deaf visually confirms color or position
6. After *"Confirmed"* — cut precisely where you are holding

### Wire Distinction Without Color

Since all wires look gray to you, distinguish them by:
- **Position** — Most reliable method
- **Thickness** — Some modules use different gauge wires
- **Texture** — Smooth vs. slightly ridged insulation

## Button Panels

For button grids, use the reference-hand technique:

1. Place your non-dominant hand on the module's edge as a fixed reference
2. With your dominant hand, count buttons by touch
3. Press each button gently to confirm position before the final press
4. Deaf confirms: *"Yes, that is the red button in row two — press and hold"*

## Common Mistakes & Fixes

| Mistake | The Fix |
|---------|---------|
| Acting before confirmation | Always repeat the instruction back first |
| Rushing under time pressure | Slow is smooth, smooth is fast — panic kills |
| Crossing module boundaries | Feel the entire edge of each module before acting |
| Silent operation | Narrate everything — touch, position, action |
| Losing spatial reference | Keep one hand on a fixed point at all times |

## Advanced Techniques

### The Anchor Hand
Always keep one hand touching a fixed reference — the bomb casing edge, a completed module, or the table surface. This prevents spatial drift.

### Tactile Memory
After touching a module once, memorize its feel. You will return to it faster the second time.

### Error Recovery
If you make a mistake:
1. Immediately remove both hands
2. Announce *"Error — hands off"*
3. Wait for new instructions
4. Never attempt to fix it on your own

## Team Communication

Your relationship with the Deaf Monkey is the most important factor in successful defusal:
- Build a rhythm of instruction → repeat → confirm → execute → report
- Your calm is contagious — so is your panic
- Trust the process: the Mute has the answers, the Deaf has the vision, you have the hands
